# The Shroud of Turin

!!! relic "First-Class Relic"
    A linen cloth bearing the image of a man who appears to have suffered physical trauma consistent with crucifixion. Kept in the Cathedral of Saint John the Baptist in Turin, Italy.

<div class="divider-sacred"></div>

## Description

The Shroud is a rectangular linen cloth measuring approximately 4.4 × 1.1 meters. It bears a faint, sepia-toned image of the front and back of a naked man with his hands folded across his groin.

<div class="divider-sacred"></div>

## Historical Record

- **First documented appearance:** 1353, displayed in the church at Lirey, France
- **Current location:** Cathedral of Saint John the Baptist, Turin, Italy
- **Custodian:** The Holy See (since 1983)

<div class="divider-sacred"></div>

## Scientific Studies

### Key Findings:

- **1988 Carbon Dating:** Suggested 1260-1390 AD (controversial)
- **Pollen Analysis:** Shows plant species from Jerusalem
- **Blood Stains:** Type AB, consistent with crucifixion wounds
- **Image Formation:** No known artistic technique explains it

<div class="divider-sacred"></div>

## Significance

The Catholic Church has never officially declared the Shroud authentic, but allows veneration. Popes have referred to it as an "icon" of the Passion.

!!! sacred "Theological Note"
    While the Church has not definitively ruled on the Shroud's authenticity, it remains one of the most studied and debated religious artifacts in history. Whether authentic burial cloth or medieval creation, it serves as a powerful reminder of Christ's suffering and sacrifice.

<div class="divider-sacred"></div>

## Veneration

The Shroud is periodically displayed to the public. During these exhibitions, millions of pilgrims visit to venerate this First-Class Relic.

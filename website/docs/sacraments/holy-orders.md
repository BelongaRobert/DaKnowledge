# Holy Orders

## The Sacrament of Apostolic Ministry

Holy Orders is the sacrament by which men are ordained as **bishops, priests, and deacons** — configured to Christ the Head and Shepherd, and entrusted with sacred authority to serve the Church.

> *"You are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God's own people, that you may declare the wonderful deeds of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light."*  
> — 1 Peter 2:9

---

## The Common Priesthood and the Ministerial Priesthood

The Church recognizes two participations in the one priesthood of Christ:

### The Common Priesthood of the Faithful
All baptized Christians share in Christ's priesthood. By their baptism, they are consecrated to offer spiritual sacrifices, to pray, and to witness. This is the **common priesthood** of all believers.

### The Ministerial Priesthood
The ordained minister — bishop, priest, or deacon — serves the common priesthood by acting **in persona Christi capitis** (in the person of Christ the Head). The ministerial priesthood is at the service of the common priesthood, not above it.

> *"The ministerial priest, by the sacred power he enjoys, teaches and rules the priestly people."*  
> — Lumen Gentium, 10

---

## The Three Degrees of Holy Orders

### 1. The Episcopate (Bishops)
Bishops receive the **fullness of the sacrament** of Holy Orders. They are successors of the apostles, with authority to teach, govern, and sanctify the universal Church. The bishop is the ordinary minister of Confirmation and Holy Orders.

### 2. The Presbyterate (Priests)
Priests are co-workers with the bishops, sharing in the bishop's mission. They preach the Gospel, celebrate the Eucharist, forgive sins, and shepherd the faithful. Priests act **in persona Christi** in the celebration of the Eucharist and the sacrament of Reconciliation.

### 3. The Diaconate (Deacons)
Deacons are ordained for **service** (diakonia). They assist the bishop and priests, proclaim the Gospel, distribute Communion, bless marriages, and serve the poor. Deacons do not celebrate Mass or hear confessions but are essential to the Church's mission.

---

## The Effects of Holy Orders

### 1. Indelible Character
Holy Orders imprints an **indelible spiritual mark** on the soul. Once ordained, a man is a priest forever (Hebrews 7:17). This character can never be erased — even if the priest is laicized, he remains ontologically a priest.

### 2. Configuration to Christ
The ordained are configured to Christ as Head and Shepherd. They act in His person, representing Him to the Church and the Church to Him.

### 3. Grace for Ministry
The sacrament confers the grace necessary for the exercise of the ordained ministry — wisdom, courage, compassion, and perseverance.

### 4. Sacred Power
The ordained receive a **sacred power** — not domination but service. This power is a gift to the Church, exercised for the good of God's people.

## The Rite of Ordination

The ordination liturgy includes:
- **Election** — the candidate is called by name and presented to the Church
- **Litany of the Saints** — the Church invokes the intercession of heaven
- **Laying on of Hands** — the bishop and other priests impose hands, invoking the Holy Spirit
- **Prayer of Consecration** — asking God to send the Holy Spirit upon the candidate
- **Investiture** — the newly ordained receives the symbols of his office (book of the Gospels, chasuble, stole, etc.)

---

## Celibacy and the Priesthood

In the Latin Church, priests (and bishops) are generally required to be **celibate**. This discipline:
- Frees the priest for total dedication to Christ and the Church
- Is a sign of the Kingdom of God, where there is no marrying or giving in marriage
- Makes the priest a living image of Christ, who was Himself celibate

In the Eastern Catholic Churches, married men may be ordained to the priesthood (though bishops are chosen from celibate priests). Both traditions honor celibacy as a gift and a sacrifice.

## The Dignity and Responsibility of the Priesthood

The priesthood is the highest dignity on earth — not because of the man but because of the office. The priest stands at the altar, re-presenting the sacrifice of Calvary; he stands in the confessional, speaking the words of absolution; he stands at the bedside of the dying, bringing Christ's peace.

Yet this dignity is also a **burden**. The priest is called to holiness, to self-sacrifice, and to total service of the flock. He is not his own; he belongs to Christ and to the Church.

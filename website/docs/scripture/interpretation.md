# Biblical Interpretation

## Hermeneutics: The Science of Understanding Scripture

Hermeneutics (from Greek *hermeneuo*, "to interpret") is the science and art of interpreting the Bible. Because the Bible is the Word of God in human language, it requires careful, prayerful, and informed reading.

> *"Do you understand what you are reading?"  > And he said, "How can I, unless someone guides me?"*  
> — Acts 8:30-31

---

## Principles of Catholic Biblical Interpretation

The Church, guided by the Holy Spirit, has established principles for reading Scripture faithfully:

### 1. Scripture is One Coherent Whole
The Bible is not a random collection of ancient texts but a unified revelation. The Old Testament prepares for the New; the New fulfills the Old. Every book must be read in light of the whole.

> *"The New Testament lies hidden in the Old, and the Old is unveiled in the New."*  
> — St. Augustine

### 2. Scripture is Read in the Church
The Bible belongs to the Church. It was written by the Church's authors, preserved by the Church's tradition, and interpreted by the Church's magisterium. Private interpretation — reading Scripture apart from the Church's guidance — can lead to error.

### 3. Scripture is Read with the Mind of the Church
The Church Fathers, the liturgy, the magisterium, and the communion of saints all provide the interpretive context. No passage should be read in isolation from the Church's teaching.

### 4. Scripture is Read Prayerfully
The Bible is not merely a text to be analyzed but a Word to be received. Lectio Divina — prayerful reading — is the Church's traditional method of approaching Scripture.

## The Four Senses of Scripture

The Church traditionally recognizes **four senses** or levels of meaning in the biblical text:

### 1. The Literal Sense
What the text actually says — the historical, grammatical, and literary meaning intended by the human author. This is the foundation of all interpretation.

> *"The literal sense is the meaning conveyed by the words of Scripture and discovered by exegesis, following the rules of sound interpretation."*  
> — Catechism of the Catholic Church, 116

### 2. The Allegorical (Typological) Sense
How the text points to Christ and the Church. Events, persons, and institutions of the Old Testament **prefigure** (are types of) Christ and the New Covenant.

Examples:
- **Adam** is a type of Christ (Romans 5:14) — the first Adam brought death; Christ, the New Adam, brings life
- **The Passover lamb** is a type of Christ (1 Corinthians 5:7) — its blood saves from death
- **The manna** is a type of the Eucharist (John 6) — bread from heaven

### 3. The Moral (Tropological) Sense
What the text teaches us about how to live. Scripture is not merely informative but **transformative** — it calls us to conversion and holiness.

> *"Whatever was written in former days was written for our instruction, that by steadfastness and by the encouragement of the scriptures we might have hope."*  
> — Romans 15:4

### 4. The Anagogical (Eschatological) Sense
What the text reveals about eternal life and the final consummation of all things. The anagogical sense draws our hearts toward heaven.

Examples:
- **The Promised Land** prefigures heaven — our true homeland
- **The Jerusalem temple** prefigures the heavenly Jerusalem (Revelation 21)
- **The wedding feast** prefigures the marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19)

## Methods of Interpretation

### Historical-Critical Method
This modern method examines the Bible using tools of history, archaeology, linguistics, and literary analysis. It can be helpful when used rightly — but it has limitations:
- It tends to focus on the human author to the neglect of the divine author
- It can reduce Scripture to merely human documents
- It must be complemented by theological and spiritual reading

### Theological Exegesis
This approach reads Scripture in light of the Church's faith — using the Creeds, the Church Fathers, the liturgy, and the magisterium as interpretive guides.

### Lectio Divina
The Church's ancient method of prayerful reading:

1. **Lectio** (Reading) — Read the text slowly, attentively
2. **Meditatio** (Meditation) — Reflect on what the text says
3. **Oratio** (Prayer) — Respond to God in conversation
4. **Contemplatio** (Contemplation) — Rest in God's presence, silent communion

Lectio Divina is not study but **encounter** — meeting the living God in His Word.

## Common Errors in Interpretation

| Error | Description | Catholic Response |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Fundamentalism** | Treating every text as a literal scientific or historical statement | Scripture uses many genres; not every text is literal |
| **Private Interpretation** | Reading apart from the Church's tradition and magisterium | Scripture belongs to the Church |
| **Reductiveive Historicism** | Reducing the text to merely human documents | Scripture is the Word of God |
| **Allegorical Excess** | Finding hidden meanings unrelated to the text | Allegory must be grounded in the literal sense |
| **Proof-Texting** | Isolating verses from context | Every text must be read in context |

## The Role of the Magisterium

The Church's teaching authority (magisterium) has the responsibility to **authentically interpret** the Word of God. This is not a tyrannical imposition but a service to the faithful — ensuring that the Gospel is understood rightly and handed down intact.

> *"The task of giving an authentic interpretation of the Word of God, whether in its written form or in the form of Tradition, has been entrusted to the living, teaching office of the Church alone."*  
> — Dei Verbum, 10

The magisterium is the servant of the Word, not its master. It guards the treasure, explains its meaning, and applies it to new circumstances — always under the guidance of the Holy Spirit.

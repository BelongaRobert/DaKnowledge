# Soteriology

## The Study of Salvation

Soteriology (from Greek *soteria*, "salvation") is the study of how God saves us — the doctrine of redemption, justification, sanctification, and glorification. It asks: **How does the work of Christ become effective in the life of the believer?** How are we rescued from sin and brought into communion with God?

> *"For God so loved the world that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life."*  
> — John 3:16

---

## The Human Condition: Sin and Its Effects

To understand salvation, we must first understand what we are saved **from**. The Church teaches that sin is not merely a moral failure but a rupture in our relationship with God.

### Original Sin
Through Adam's transgression, humanity lost original holiness and justice. We are born into a state of privation — lacking the grace that Adam and Eve possessed. This does not mean every human act is sinful, but it means our nature is wounded and inclined toward sin.

> *"Sin came into the world through one man and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all men sinned."*  
> — Romans 5:12

### Actual Sin
Beyond the inherited wound of original sin, each person commits **actual sins** — thoughts, words, deeds, and omissions contrary to God's law. These sins further damage our relationship with God and neighbor.

### The Effects of Sin
- **Separation from God** — sin breaks communion
- **Loss of grace** — sanctifying grace is expelled from the soul
- **Wounding of nature** — the intellect darkens, the will weakens, the passions rebel
- **Eternal consequences** — unrepented mortal sin results in eternal separation from God (hell)

---

## Salvation: The Work of Grace

Salvation is entirely God's gift. We do not earn it, achieve it, or deserve it. It flows from the free mercy of God and the meritorious work of Christ.

> *"By grace you have been saved through faith; and this is not your own doing, it is the gift of God — not because of works, lest any man should boast."*  
> — Ephesians 2:8-9

Yet salvation is not merely a legal declaration. It is a **transformation** — we are made new, restored to grace, adopted as children of God, and destined for glory.

## The Order of Salvation (Ordo Salutis)

While the exact sequence varies among theologians, the Catholic understanding of salvation includes these elements:

1. **Election** — God chooses us in Christ before the foundation of the world (Ephesians 1:4)
2. **Calling** — The Gospel is preached; the Spirit draws the heart (John 6:44)
3. **Conversion** — Repentance and faith; turning from sin to God (Acts 3:19)
4. **Justification** — Forgiveness of sins and infusion of grace (Romans 3:24)
5. **Adoption** — We become children of God (1 John 3:1)
6. **Sanctification** — Growth in holiness through the Spirit (1 Thessalonians 4:3)
7. **Perseverance** — Remaining in grace to the end (Matthew 24:13)
8. **Glorification** — Resurrection and eternal life with God (Romans 8:30)

These are not separate, disconnected events but aspects of one great work of grace. In Baptism, for example, justification, adoption, and the beginning of sanctification occur simultaneously.

---

## Faith and Works

The Catholic Church teaches that salvation is **by grace alone** but that grace necessarily produces **good works**. Faith without works is dead (James 2:26). Good works are not the cause of salvation but the **fruit** of grace already received.

> *"We are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them."*  
> — Ephesians 2:10

The works done in grace are themselves meritorious — not by our own power but by the power of Christ working in us. This is the mystery of **cooperation** (*synergia*): God works, and we work; His grace enables our free response.

# Justification

## Being Made Right with God

Justification is the gracious act of God by which He forgives our sins and makes us righteous — not by imputing an external righteousness but by **infusing** His own divine life into our souls. In justification, we are truly transformed, not merely declared innocent.

> *"Since we are justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ."*  
> — Romans 5:1

---

## Justification as Transformation

The Catholic Church teaches that justification is not only a **forensic** (legal) declaration but a **real change** in the soul. When God justifies the sinner, He does not merely pretend the sinner is righteous; He **makes** the sinner righteous by grace.

> *"Christ Jesus... became for us wisdom from God, and righteousness and sanctification and redemption."*  
> — 1 Corinthians 1:30

This does not mean we become righteous by our own efforts. It means that the righteousness of Christ becomes ours by grace — infused into our souls, making us truly just in God's sight.

---

## The Causes of Justification

St. Thomas Aquinas and the Catholic tradition distinguish the various causes:

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **Final Cause** | The glory of God and of Christ |
| **Efficient Cause** | The mercy of God; the merit of Christ's passion and resurrection |
| **Meritorious Cause** | Christ's sacrifice on the cross |
| **Instrumental Cause** | The sacraments, especially Baptism |
| **Formal Cause** | The grace of God and the charity infused into the heart |

The formal cause is key: the righteousness by which we are justified is not merely an external imputation but an **intrinsic, supernatural quality** — sanctifying grace and the theological virtue of charity.

## Faith and Justification

Faith is the **beginning** of justification, the **foundation**, and the **root** of all that follows. But faith, to be saving faith, must be **living faith** — faith working through love (Galatians 5:6).

> *"You see that a man is justified by works and not by faith alone."*  
> — James 2:24

This does not contradict Paul's teaching that we are justified by faith apart from works of the Law (Romans 3:28). Paul is arguing against the notion that external observances (circumcision, dietary laws) can justify. James is arguing against a dead, intellectual faith that produces no fruit. Together, they teach: **we are justified by grace through faith, and that faith must be alive in love and good works.**

## The Process of Justification

### For Adults
For those who reach the age of reason, justification involves:

1. **Preparation** — The grace of God touches the heart, producing initial faith and repentance
2. **Conversion** — Turning from sin to God (metanoia)
3. **Contrition** — Sorrow for sin, with the resolution to amend
4. **Confession** — Acknowledging sins (in Baptism, this is implicit; in the sacrament of Reconciliation, explicit)
5. **Forgiveness** — God pardons sins and blots them out
6. **Infusion of Grace** — Sanctifying grace, the theological virtues (faith, hope, charity), and the gifts of the Spirit are poured into the soul

### In Baptism
Baptism is the **ordinary means** of justification. In Baptism:
- Original sin is remitted
- All actual sins are forgiven
- Sanctifying grace is infused
- The theological virtues are given
- The person is incorporated into Christ and the Church

> *"He saved us, not because of deeds done by us in righteousness, but in virtue of his own mercy, by the washing of regeneration and renewal in the Holy Spirit."*  
> — Titus 3:5

## The Justice of Justification

Some worry that justification by grace undermines God's justice. If God forgives freely, does He ignore sin? The answer is no: **God's justice is fully satisfied in Christ.**

The cross is the place where mercy and justice meet. The Son bears the punishment we deserve, making it possible for the Father to forgive without compromising His holiness. Christ's merits are the ground of our justification; grace is the means.

> *"For our sake he made him to be sin who knew no sin, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God."*  
> — 2 Corinthians 5:21

---

## Growth in Justification

Justification is not a one-time event after which we remain unchanged. The justified person grows in grace, cooperates with the Spirit, and is strengthened by the sacraments.

Yet the justified person can also **lose grace** through mortal sin. This is why the Church teaches the necessity of perseverance, ongoing conversion, and the sacrament of Reconciliation for those who fall.

> *"Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling; for God is at work in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure."*  
> — Philippians 2:12-13
